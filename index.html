Part 1: BACKGROUND
Why we need Colocalisation?

Let's start with the biological motivation. Before we even think about running any software, we need to ask: why do we need colocalisation in the first place? In post-GWAS interpretation, it's a very familiar temptation to see a disease signal and an eQTL signal right next to each other, and immediately assume that the gene is causal. That intuition is a great starting point, but it's not enough evidence. As we'll see in the next few slides, nearby peaks can reflect a shared variant, but they can also just be different variants in LD.

Do these two traits share a causal variant in this region?

So, this is the core question of the entire presentation: Do these two traits share a single causal variant in this specific genomic region? We are not just asking if the two association plots look similar visually. We are asking a much more specific statistical question about shared causality. If you keep this exact question in mind, the rest of the COLOC method will make a lot more sense.

What colocalisation asks?

  • Are both traits associated in this region?
  • If yes, are they more consistent with one shared causal variant than with two distinct variants?
  • How strong is that support after accounting for uncertainty?
To answer that, colocalisation breaks the problem down into three sequential questions. First, is there even an association for Trait 1 in this region? And is there one for Trait 2? If not, there's nothing to colocalise. Second, if both are associated, is the overall pattern of association across the whole region more consistent with one shared causal variant, or with two completely distinct causal variants? And third, how confident are we in that conclusion, after accounting for all the uncertainty across all the SNPs in the locus?

Why not just look at lead SNPs or fine-mapping results?

  • Lead SNPs can differ across traits due to LD and noise.
  • Nearby peaks can reflect shared or distinct causal variants.
  • Fine-mapping methods like SuSiE may not powerful enough to find the exact shared SNP.
So why not just look at the lead SNPs? Or just run fine-mapping? This is exactly why visual overlap or matching lead SNPs is not enough. LD can make two different causal variants look incredibly similar. On the flip side, sampling noise can make the lead SNP differ between the two traits even when the underlying causal variant is exactly the same. Fine-mapping helps, but it doesn't explicitly answer the cross-trait question. COLOC is powerful because it explicitly compares these two explanations: shared versus distinct causal variants.

Colocalisation: A Powerful Tool

  • It quantifies the evidence for shared / distinct causal variants in a region.
  • It does not need to find the causal variants.
  • It has direct implications for the understanding of complex diseases and design of drugs to target disease pathways.
To summarize this first part, colocalisation is a powerful tool because it quantifies the evidence for shared versus distinct causal variants in a region. It moves us away from asking whether the two plots look similar, and instead asks whether both traits are consistent with the same causal variant in this region.

Where we can use colocalisation?

  • GWAS × GWAS
    Trait vs. Trait
  • GWAS × QTL
    Disease vs. Regulatory Mechanism (e.g. expression)
  • QTL × QTL
    Molecular vs. Molecular (e.g. eQTL, meQTL, pQTL)
So where can we actually use colocalisation? It's important to remember that COLOC isn't just restricted to comparing a GWAS against an eQTL. We can compare two different GWAS traits, or two molecular traits, or any combination. As long as both traits are measured in the same genomic region and we want to ask whether they share causal architecture, the framework applies. The key requirement is that we are asking a regional causal question.

Part 2:
COLOC workflow

Okay, now that we understand the motivation, let's move on to Part 2: the actual COLOC workflow. If you take away one practical pipeline from this talk, it should be the standard COLOC.abf workflow. I'll first walk you through the original model, then the required inputs, and finally how to interpret the output in a sensible order.

The original COLOC (2014) model

Assumption: at most one causal variant per trait in the region

  • H0: 1❌ 2❌.
  • H1: 1 ✅ 2❌.
  • H2: 1 ❌ 2 ✅.
  • H3: 1 ✅ 2 ✅ different signals.
  • H4: 1 ✅ 2 ✅ shared signals.

Main output: posterior probabilities (PP) for H0 to H4, especially PP.H4.

This is the classical 2014 COLOC framework. Its appeal is that it's simple, fast, and gives us five mutually exclusive hypotheses, from H0 to H4. H0 means neither trait is associated. H1 and H2 mean only one trait is associated. H3 means both are associated, but with distinct causal variants. And H4 is the one we usually care about: both traits are associated and share a single causal variant. The crucial assumption here is that each trait has *at most one* causal variant in the region. That assumption makes interpretation easy, but it's also the main limitation we need to keep in mind for later.

What data does COLOC need?

Requirement

  • Two traits, one genomic region.
  • Common set of dense SNPs in both studies.
  • Case-control or Quantitative trait.
  • Recommended inputs: beta + varbeta.

Common mistakes

  • Not aligning the directions of effect.
  • Using a whole chromosome or genome.
  • Merging several genes into one eQTL dataset.
  • Using only the lead SNPs.
This is one of the most practical slides in the talk, because many bad analyses fail before COLOC is even run. The method expects two traits, one bounded region, a common dense SNP set, and properly harmonized summary statistics. If you trim your data down to just a few lead SNPs, you remove exactly the regional pattern that COLOC needs in order to compare the two traits.

COLOC model

Posterior probability of H4:

$$ \begin{aligned} \text{PPH4} & =P\left(H_4 \mid D\right) \\ & =\frac{P\left(H_4 \mid D\right)}{P\left(H_0 \mid D\right)+P\left(H_1 \mid D\right)+P\left(H_2 \mid D\right)+P\left(H_3 \mid D\right)+P\left(H_4 \mid D\right)} \\ & =\frac{\frac{P\left(H_4 \mid D\right)}{P\left(H_0 \mid D\right)}}{1+\frac{P\left(H_1 \mid D\right)}{P\left(H_0 \mid D\right)}+\frac{P\left(H_2 \mid D\right)}{P\left(H_0 \mid D\right)}+\frac{P\left(H_3 \mid D\right)}{P\left(H_0 \mid D\right)}+\frac{P\left(H_4 \mid D\right)}{P\left(H_0 \mid D\right)}} \end{aligned} $$

Bayes factor ratio:

$$ \frac{P(H_h \mid D)}{P(H_0 \mid D)} = \sum_{S \in S_h} \underbrace{\frac{P(D \mid S)}{P(D \mid S_0)}}_{\color{red}{\text{Bayes Factor (BF)}}} \times \underbrace{\frac{P(S)}{P(S_0)}}_{\color{red}{\text{Prior odds}}} $$
Here is the COLOC model. PPH4 is the posterior probability of H4. PPH4 is calculated by the Bayes factor ratio of H4 to H0. The Bayes factor ratio is calculated by the ABF of each SNP.

Calculation of ABF

For the $j^{th}$ SNP, we use Wakefield's method to calculate the Approximate Bayes Factor (ABF) of association with trait $T$:

$$ \text{ABF}_j^T = \sqrt{1-r} \times \exp\left[\frac{Z_j^2}{2} \times r\right] $$
  • $Z$ (Wald test statistic): $Z = \hat{\beta}/\sqrt{V}$.
  • $W$ (Prior variance): The prior variance of the true effect size. Following default specifications: $W = 0.15^2$ for continuous quantitative traits and $W = 0.2^2$ for case-control studies.
  • $r$ (Shrinkage factor): $r = \frac{W}{V + W}$. This factor ranges from 0 to 1 and measures the relative contribution of the prior ($W$) versus data likelihood ($V$). As sample size increases, $V \to 0$ and $r \to 1$.
I don't expect you to memorize this equation, but I do want you to understand the intuition behind it. For each SNP, the Approximate Bayes Factor (or ABF) measures how much more the data support an association compared to the null, while also incorporating a prior on plausible effect sizes. So the key idea is that COLOC starts from SNP-level evidence and then aggregates that evidence into region-level hypotheses.

Calculation of PPH4

$$\text{PPH4} = \frac{\frac{P\left(H_4 \mid D\right)}{P\left(H_0 \mid D\right)}}{1+\frac{P\left(H_1 \mid D\right)}{P\left(H_0 \mid D\right)}+\frac{P\left(H_2 \mid D\right)}{P\left(H_0 \mid D\right)}+\frac{P\left(H_3 \mid D\right)}{P\left(H_0 \mid D\right)}+\frac{P\left(H_4 \mid D\right)}{P\left(H_0 \mid D\right)}}.$$ $$ \begin{aligned} \frac{P(H_1|D)}{P(H_0|D)} & = p_1 \sum_{j=1}^{Q} \text{ABF}_{j}^{1},\quad \frac{P(H_2|D)}{P(H_0|D)} = p_2 \sum_{j=1}^{Q} \text{ABF}_{j}^{2}, \\ \frac{P(H_3|D)}{P(H_0|D)} & = p_1 p_2 \sum_{j \neq k} \text{ABF}_{j}^{1} \times \text{ABF}_{k}^{2},\quad \frac{P(H_4|D)}{P(H_0|D)} = p_{12} \sum_{j=1}^{Q} \text{ABF}_{j}^{1} \times \text{ABF}_{j}^{2}. \end{aligned} $$
Here is the calculation of PPH4. PPH4 is the posterior probability of H4. PPH4 is calculated by the Bayes factor ratio of H4 to H0. The Bayes factor ratio is calculated by the ABF of each SNP.

A practical baseline workflow

  1. Define a single candidate locus.
  2. Harmonize the SNP set across the two studies.
  3. (Optional) Build COLOC datasets and check them.
  4. Run COLOC.abf().
  5. Read the regional posterior summary.
  6. (Optional) Inspect SNP-level support if H4 is convincing.

Useful functions in the tutorials: check_dataset(), COLOC.abf(), sensitivity().

Here is the minimum practical workflow. First, define a single locus. Second, harmonize the SNP set between your two studies. Third, if needed, use the helper functions to validate your inputs. Fourth, run COLOC.abf. And finally, interpret the result in the right order: always start with the regional posterior probabilities, and only then look at the SNP-level quantities.

How to read the output correctly

Regional layer

  • PP.H4: support for one shared causal variant in the region.
  • PP.H3: support for two distinct causal variants in the region.
  • Interpret this layer first.

SNP layer

  • SNP.PP.H4: which SNP is most likely shared, conditional on H4.
  • High PP.H4 can still coexist with uncertainty about the exact SNP.
  • Regional colocalisation is not identical to SNP resolution.
This distinction is really worth pausing on. PP.H4 is a regional statement: do the data support one shared causal variant *somewhere* in the locus? SNP.PP.H4 is a conditional statement: *if* H4 is true, which specific SNP is the best candidate? Strong regional colocalisation does not guarantee precise SNP resolution, because multiple variants can remain statistically similar due to LD. You can be very confident they share a variant, without knowing exactly which variant it is.

Priors are important!

COLOC uses three per-SNP prior probabilities:

  • p1: the SNP is causal for trait 1.
  • p2: the SNP is causal for trait 2.
  • p12: the SNP is causal for both traits.

Important idea: priors are specified per SNP, but posterior conclusions are interpreted at the region level.

This is where many analyses become more subjective than people realize. Priors are specified per SNP, but our conclusions are usually discussed at the region level. That means locus size and prior choice interact. In a larger region, H3—the hypothesis of two distinct causal variants—can become relatively more competitive simply because there are many more ways to choose two distinct variants than there are to choose one shared one.

Prior: SNP vs. Region

Sensitivity analysis of priors

Sensitivity analysis is not just an optional technical extra. It tells us whether our conclusion is robust, or whether it depends heavily on one arbitrary prior setting. If a strong H4 result remains stable across a wide range of reasonable priors, that is much more convincing than a result that changes immediately when you tweak the parameters slightly.

Part 3: coloc.susie
When one signal per trait
is not enough

Up to this point, the story assumed at most one causal variant per trait in the region. That assumption is convenient, but biology is often more complicated than that. Once a locus contains multiple signals, standard COLOC.abf can become misleading. That is why we need coloc.susie.

Where the single-causal assumption breaks

A locus may contain multiple signals, but only one of them is shared.

This slide explains the failure mode. A locus may contain multiple signals, but only one of them is shared across traits. In that setting, forcing the whole locus into a single-signal model can blur the interpretation. Historically people used conditioning or masking, but the cleaner modern answer is to model multiple signals directly.

Where the single-causal assumption breaks

  • A locus may contain multiple signals and some of them are shared across traits.
  • COLOC ignores LD structure.
  • Historically, conditioning was used to relax the assumption.
  • Today, the recommended solution is to use SuSiE + COLOC.
This slide explains exactly why the single-variant assumption fails. A single locus can easily contain one signal that is shared across both traits, and another signal that is specific to only one trait. If you force that entire region into a single-signal model, the interpretation gets blurred. Historically, people tried to solve this with conditional analysis or masking, but the cleaner, modern answer is to model multiple signals directly from the start.

Illustration of SuSiE-based COLOC

What coloc.susie() does

So how does coloc.susie work? The key idea is to decompose the locus first, and compare signals second. First, SuSiE separates each trait into multiple single-effect components. Then, COLOC evaluates pairs of these signals across the two traits. Conceptually, this is much closer to what we think complex loci actually look like biologically. The trade-off is that we now require LD information, and the interpretation requires a bit more care.

Part 4: Variant-Specific Priors
Next steps for Colocalisation

In this final part, I want to introduce a newer extension. Variant-specific priors don't redefine the core concept of colocalisation. Instead, they refine the prior model by allowing some variants to start with more prior weight than others, based on external biological information.

Variant-specific priors

Variant-specific priors

  • Specify prior_weights1 and prior_weights2 in COLOC.abf() or coloc.susie().
  • Intuitively, not all variants have the same prior of being causal.
  • Examples: functional annotations, PolyFun-style priors, or eQTL-TSS distance priors.
  • Such priors can improve performance, especially with eQTL-TSS distance density priors (variants that lie closer to the TSS are more likely to be causal).
  • For each gene, measure the distance from its top eQTL to the TSS, then use KDE to estimate the prior distribution.
I like to present this as a practical refinement, not a magic fix. If you have biological annotations, fine-mapping-informed weights, or even just distance-to-TSS, you can use that information to make your prior more realistic. The gains are usually modest rather than revolutionary, but they can still improve calibration and help resolve borderline cases.

Variant-Specific Priors Change Conclusion

Example: GWAS signal across PSMB7 and NEK6 because of the extended LD.

  • Using coloc-single, this signal strongly colocalised with eQTL associations for both genes in GTEx (d).
  • GWAS signal is spread across both genes, though the peak is nearer the NEK6 TSS than the PSMB7 TSS.
  • In eQTL, peaks for each gene are close to their respective TSSs (b/c).
  • When using a NEK6 TSS–centered prior in GWAS–NEK6 colocalisation, the signal remains significant, but using a PSMB7 TSS–centered prior for GWAS–PSMB7 colocalisation, PPH4 drops to 0.42.
  • Therefore, incorporating prior information at this locus changes the colocalisation conclusion to strongly prefer NEK6 as the causal gene.
Here is a great example of the practical impact of using variant-specific priors. In a region with extended LD, the standard model might be ambiguous. But by upweighting variants close to the most plausible Transcription Start Site, we can shift the statistical support from multiple candidate genes to the most biologically likely one, resolving the ambiguity.

Take-home messages

  • COLOC tests shared causal variation in a region, not just overlap of top SNPs.
  • Data preparation and priors matter; sensitivity analysis could be helpful.
  • For complex loci, coloc.susie is the key upgrade; variant-specific priors are a useful refinement.
To wrap up, I want to return to our main narrative. First, COLOC is about testing for shared causal variation in a region, not just looking for overlapping peaks. Second, data preparation and sensible priors matter immensely. And third, when loci are clearly complex, coloc.susie is the essential upgrade. Ultimately, the story of this method is really the story of making our causal interpretations more rigorous and honest.
