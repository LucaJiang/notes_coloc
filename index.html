Colocalisation:
From Association Signals to Shared Causal Variants

Wenxin Jiang

print pdf version

Today I will give a practical introduction to COLOC. I assume the audience already knows what GWAS and QTL studies are, but may not yet feel comfortable with how colocalisation is framed and interpreted. I will move from the biological question, to the standard COLOC workflow, to why priors matter, and finally to what changes when one signal per trait is not enough. My goal is not to cover every implementation detail. My goal is that by the end of this talk, the logic of COLOC feels intuitive and the workflow feels usable.

Part 1: BACKGROUND
Why we need Colocalisation?

I want to start with the biological motivation rather than the software. In practice, people often see a disease signal and an eQTL signal in the same region and immediately want to connect them mechanistically. That intuition is useful, but it is not yet evidence of a shared causal variant. The next few slides explain why that distinction matters.

Do these two traits share a causal variant in this region?

This is the central question of the whole presentation. We are not asking whether the two plots look similar at a glance. We are asking whether both traits are consistent with the same causal variant in this region. If you keep that question in mind, the rest of the method becomes much easier to understand.

What colocalisation asks?

  • Are both traits associated in this region?
  • If yes, are they more consistent with one shared causal variant than with two distinct variants?
  • How strong is that support after accounting for uncertainty?
Colocalisation asks three things in sequence. First, is trait 1 associated in this region, and is trait 2 also associated in this region? Second, if both are associated, is the overall pattern more consistent with one shared causal variant or with two distinct causal variants? Third, how confident are we after accounting for uncertainty across all SNPs in the locus?

Why not just look at lead SNPs or fine-mapping results?

  • Lead SNPs can differ across traits due to LD and noise.
  • Nearby peaks can reflect shared or distinct causal variants.
  • Fine-mapping methods like SuSiE may not powerful enough to find the exact shared SNP.
This is why visual overlap or matching lead SNPs is not enough. LD can make different causal variants look similar, and sampling noise can make the lead SNP differ even when the underlying causal variant is the same. Fine-mapping helps, but it does not by itself answer the cross-trait question. COLOC is useful because it compares these explanations explicitly.

Where we can use colocalisation?

This slide broadens the use case. Colocalisation is not restricted to GWAS against eQTL. We can compare two GWAS traits, or two molecular traits, as long as they are measured in the same genomic region and we want to ask whether they share causal architecture. The framework is general; the key requirement is the regional causal question.

Molecular Quantitative Trait Locus (molQTL)

molQTL mapping links genetic variants to molecular phenotypes (e.g., expression, methylation).

Part 2:
COLOC workflow

Now we move from motivation to workflow. If the audience remembers one practical pipeline from this talk, it should be the standard COLOC.abf workflow. I will first show the original model, then the required inputs, and then how to interpret the output in a sensible order.

The original COLOC (2014) model

Assumption: at most one causal variant per trait in the region

  • H0: 1❌ 2❌.
  • H1: 1 ✅ 2❌.
  • H2: 1 ❌ 2 ✅.
  • H3: 1 ✅ 2 ✅ different signals.
  • H4: 1 ✅ 2 ✅ shared signals.

Main output: posterior probabilities (PP) for H0 to H4, especially PP.H4.

This is the classical 2014 COLOC framework. Its appeal is that it is simple, fast, and gives five mutually exclusive hypotheses from H0 to H4. The crucial assumption is that each trait has at most one causal variant in the region. That assumption makes interpretation easy, but it is also the main limitation we need to remember later.

COLOC: A Real Data Example

What data does COLOC need?

Requirement

  • Two traits, one genomic region.
  • Common set of dense SNPs in both studies.
  • Case-control or Quantitative trait.
  • Recommended inputs: beta + varbeta.

Common mistakes

  • Not aligning the directions of effect.
  • Using a whole chromosome or genome.
  • Merging several genes into one eQTL dataset.
  • Using only the lead SNPs.
This is one of the most practical slides in the talk because many bad analyses fail before COLOC is even run. The method expects two traits, one bounded region, a common dense SNP set, and properly harmonized summary statistics. If you trim the data down to a few lead SNPs, you remove exactly the regional pattern that COLOC needs in order to compare the two traits.

COLOC model

Posterior probability of H4:

$$ \begin{aligned} \text{PPH4} & =P\left(H_4 \mid D\right) \\ & =\frac{P\left(H_4 \mid D\right)}{P\left(H_0 \mid D\right)+P\left(H_1 \mid D\right)+P\left(H_2 \mid D\right)+P\left(H_3 \mid D\right)+P\left(H_4 \mid D\right)} \\ & =\frac{\frac{P\left(H_4 \mid D\right)}{P\left(H_0 \mid D\right)}}{1+\frac{P\left(H_1 \mid D\right)}{P\left(H_0 \mid D\right)}+\frac{P\left(H_2 \mid D\right)}{P\left(H_0 \mid D\right)}+\frac{P\left(H_3 \mid D\right)}{P\left(H_0 \mid D\right)}+\frac{P\left(H_4 \mid D\right)}{P\left(H_0 \mid D\right)}} \end{aligned} $$

Bayes factor ratio:

$$ \frac{P(H_h \mid D)}{P(H_0 \mid D)} = \sum_{S \in S_h} \underbrace{\frac{P(D \mid S)}{P(D \mid S_0)}}_{\color{red}{\text{Bayes Factor (BF)}}} \times \underbrace{\frac{P(S)}{P(S_0)}}_{\color{red}{\text{Prior odds}}} $$

Calculation of ABF

For the $j^{th}$ SNP, we use Wakefield's method to calculate the Approximate Bayes Factor (ABF) of association with trait $T$:

$$ \text{ABF}_j^T = \sqrt{1-r} \times \exp\left[\frac{Z_j^2}{2} \times r\right] $$
  • $Z$ (Wald test statistic): $Z = \hat{\beta}/\sqrt{V}$.
  • $W$ (Prior variance): The prior variance of the true effect size. Following default specifications: $W = 0.15^2$ for continuous quantitative traits and $W = 0.2^2$ for case-control studies.
  • $r$ (Shrinkage factor): $r = \frac{W}{V + W}$. This factor ranges from 0 to 1 and measures the relative contribution of the prior ($W$) versus data likelihood ($V$). As sample size increases, $V \to 0$ and $r \to 1$.
I do not want the audience to memorize this equation, but I do want them to understand the intuition. For each SNP, the ABF measures how much more the data support association than the null, while also incorporating a prior on plausible effect sizes. So the key idea is that COLOC starts from SNP-level evidence and then aggregates that evidence into region-level hypotheses.

Calculation of PPH4

$$\text{PPH4} = \frac{\frac{P\left(H_4 \mid D\right)}{P\left(H_0 \mid D\right)}}{1+\frac{P\left(H_1 \mid D\right)}{P\left(H_0 \mid D\right)}+\frac{P\left(H_2 \mid D\right)}{P\left(H_0 \mid D\right)}+\frac{P\left(H_3 \mid D\right)}{P\left(H_0 \mid D\right)}+\frac{P\left(H_4 \mid D\right)}{P\left(H_0 \mid D\right)}}.$$ $$ \begin{aligned} \frac{P(H_1|D)}{P(H_0|D)} & = p_1 \sum_{j=1}^{Q} \text{ABF}_{j}^{1},\quad \frac{P(H_2|D)}{P(H_0|D)} = p_2 \sum_{j=1}^{Q} \text{ABF}_{j}^{2}, \\ \frac{P(H_3|D)}{P(H_0|D)} & = p_1 p_2 \sum_{j \neq k} \text{ABF}_{j}^{1} \times \text{ABF}_{k}^{2},\quad \frac{P(H_4|D)}{P(H_0|D)} = p_{12} \sum_{j=1}^{Q} \text{ABF}_{j}^{1} \times \text{ABF}_{j}^{2}. \end{aligned} $$

A practical baseline workflow

  1. Define a single candidate locus.
  2. Harmonize the SNP set across the two studies.
  3. (Optional) Build COLOC datasets and check them.
  4. Run COLOC.abf().
  5. Read the regional posterior summary.
  6. (Optional) Inspect SNP-level support only if H4 is convincing.

Useful functions in the tutorials: check_dataset(), COLOC.abf(), sensitivity().

This is the minimum practical workflow. First define a single locus. Second harmonize the SNP set between studies. Third, if needed, use the helper functions to validate the inputs. Fourth, run COLOC.abf. Finally, interpret the result in the right order: start with the regional posterior, and only then look at SNP-level quantities.

How to read the output correctly

Regional layer

  • PP.H4: support for one shared causal variant in the region.
  • PP.H3: support for two distinct causal variants in the region.
  • Interpret this layer first.

SNP layer

  • SNP.PP.H4: which SNP is most likely shared, conditional on H4.
  • High PP.H4 can still coexist with uncertainty about the exact SNP.
  • Regional colocalisation is not identical to SNP resolution.
This distinction is worth pausing on. PP.H4 is a regional statement: do the data support one shared causal variant somewhere in the locus? SNP.PP.H4 is a conditional statement: if H4 is true, which SNP is the best candidate? Strong regional colocalisation does not guarantee precise SNP resolution, because multiple variants can remain statistically similar in LD.

Priors are important!

COLOC uses three per-SNP prior probabilities:

  • p1: the SNP is causal for trait 1.
  • p2: the SNP is causal for trait 2.
  • p12: the SNP is causal for both traits.

Important idea: priors are specified per SNP, but posterior conclusions are interpreted at the region level.

This is where many analyses become more subjective than people realize. Priors are specified per SNP, but the conclusions are often discussed at the region level. That means locus size and prior choice interact. In a larger region, H3 can become relatively more competitive simply because there are many more ways to choose two distinct causal variants than one shared one.

Prior: SNP vs. Region

Sensitivity analysis of priors

Sensitivity analysis is not just a technical extra. It tells us whether our conclusion is robust or whether it depends heavily on one arbitrary prior setting. If a strong H4 result remains stable across reasonable priors, that is much more convincing than a result that changes immediately when p12 moves.

Part 3: coloc.susie
When one signal per trait
is not enough

Up to this point, the story assumed at most one causal variant per trait in the region. That assumption is convenient, but biology is often more complicated than that. Once a locus contains multiple signals, standard COLOC.abf can become misleading. That is why we need coloc.susie.

Where the single-causal assumption breaks

  • A locus may contain one shared signal and one extra trait-specific signal.
  • COLOC.abf() ignores LD structure.
  • Historically, conditioning and masking were used to relax the assumption.
  • Today, the recommended solution is to use SuSiE-based COLOC.
This slide explains the failure mode. A locus can contain one signal that is shared across traits and another signal that is trait-specific. In that setting, forcing the whole locus into a single-signal model can blur the interpretation. Historically people used conditioning or masking, but the cleaner modern answer is to model multiple signals directly.

Illustration of SuSiE-based COLOC

What coloc.susie() does

The key idea is to decompose the locus first, and compare signals second. SuSiE separates each trait into multiple single-effect components, and then COLOC evaluates pairs of signals across traits. Conceptually, this is much closer to what we think complex loci actually look like. The trade-off is that we now need LD information and slightly more careful interpretation.

Part 4: Variant-Specific Priors
Next steps for Colocalisation

In the last part, I want to introduce a newer extension without making it sound more dramatic than it is. Variant-specific priors do not redefine colocalisation. They refine the prior model by allowing some variants to start with more prior weight than others based on external information.

Variant-specific priors

Variant-specific priors

  • Use prior_weights1 and prior_weights2 in COLOC.abf() or coloc.susie().
  • Not all variants need the same prior probability of being causal.
  • Examples: functional annotations, PolyFun-style priors, or eQTL-TSS distance priors.
  • The 2025 paper found modest but useful gains, especially with eQTL-TSS distance density priors.
I would present this as a practical refinement, not a magic fix. If you have biological annotation, fine-mapping-informed weights, or distance-based priors, you can use that information to make the prior more realistic. The gains are usually modest rather than revolutionary, but they can still improve calibration and change borderline decisions in useful ways.

Take-home messages

  • COLOC tests shared causal variation in a region, not just overlap of top SNPs.
  • Data preparation and priors matter; sensitivity analysis should be routine.
  • For complex loci, coloc.susie is the key upgrade; variant-specific priors are a useful refinement.
To close, I would return to the main narrative. COLOC is about shared causal variation in a region, not just overlap of peaks. Good input data and sensible priors matter. And when loci are clearly more complex, coloc.susie is the important upgrade. So the story of the method is really the story of making causal interpretation more honest.
